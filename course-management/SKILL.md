# Course Management (DECADE System)

> **Background and theory**: Read [references/foundation.md](references/foundation.md)


## One-Line Summary

**Aim at the "safe miss zone," not the pin.** By selecting targets based on your dispersion pattern (miss pattern), you can save 2–5 strokes without changing your swing.


## Strategy by Hole Type

### Par 3
- **Safe strategy**: Center of green (or the largest area of the green)
- **Pin near hazard**: Target the opposite side of the green
- **Uncertain about distance**: Take one club more (short misses are usually more dangerous than long misses)
- **Handicap 20+**: Getting on the green is a success

### Par 4
- **Tee shot**: Widest part of the fairway + favorable angle for the approach
- Driver vs. iron/hybrid: Use the safer club when OB/hazard risk is present
- **Approach**: Center of green. GIR equals success
- **Handicap 20+ Par 4 goal**: Bogey is the baseline. Par is a bonus

### Par 5
- **Going for the green in two — criteria**: Remaining distance + hazards + success rate
  - Handicap 10 or lower: Consider attempting within 220 yards
  - Handicap 20+: Laying up is almost always the wiser choice
- **Layup position**: Your most comfortable wedge distance (80–100 yards)
- **Layup trap**: "Let me hit it a bit farther" → awkward distance → difficult approach


## Adjustments by Conditions

### Wind
- **Headwind**: Take 1–2 extra clubs, lower trajectory (grip down)
- **Tailwind**: More roll-out; land in front of the green
- **Crosswind**: Aim opposite to the wind (by your dispersion amount)
- **Korean coastal courses (Jeju, Busan)**: Wind can create 3–4 club differences

### Lies
- **Uphill lie**: Take one extra club; ball tends to go left
- **Downhill lie**: Take one less club; ball tends to go right
- **Ball above feet (sidehill)**: Ball tends to go right
- **Ball below feet**: Ball tends to go left

### Course Conditions
- **Wet fairways**: Less roll-out; take one extra club
- **Firm greens**: Take one less club + land in front of the green
- **Thick rough**: Distance loss of 20–40%; take more club + prioritize getting out


## Working with Caddies (Korea-Specific)

Most Korean golf courses require a caddie:

### What to Ask Your Caddie
- "Where is the dangerous miss from here?"
- "Which side of this green is safer?"
- "What club do players usually hit from this distance?"
- Perceived slope/wind (a caddie's on-course experience is invaluable)

### Caddie Advice vs. Your Own Judgment
- **Distance information**: Trust the caddie (they play the same course daily)
- **Club selection**: Judge based on your own distances (the caddie doesn't know your club-by-club yardages)
- **Line reading**: Use the caddie's input as reference, but make the final call yourself


## Characteristics of Korean Golf Courses

- **Predominantly mountainous courses**: Significant elevation changes, many blind holes → caddie information is crucial
- **Green speed**: 9–10 feet is typical (slower compared to U.S. courses)
- **Course length**: 6,000–6,400 yards from regular tees is standard
- **Layout**: Courses carved from mountains → frequent sloped lies
- **Spring/Fall vs. Summer**: Thicker rough in summer → increased value of hitting fairways


## Anti-Patterns

- **Always aiming at the pin** — Pins are often placed in trap positions
- **Attempting "hero shots"** — Through trees, over water, clearing bunkers → double bogey factory
- **Competing for driving distance** — Out-driving your playing partners does not contribute to your score
- **Unplanned layup distance** — Hitting "somewhere out there" without calculating the remaining distance
- **Forcing recovery after a bad hole** — Chasing birdie after a bogey → chain of double bogeys


## Limitations

1. Course management **presupposes execution ability** — Even the best strategy has limits if the shots aren't there
2. Dispersion varies day to day — Fluctuates with physical condition and mental state
3. Can be influenced by playing partners' pace/atmosphere (e.g., business golf, social pressure)
